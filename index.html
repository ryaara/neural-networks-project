Predicting Regions of Climate Change-Induced Drought

Ryan O'Hara

Project Description

Much of modern climate change research is built on Earth system models (ESMs), bottom-up simulations of future climate activity based on the laws of physics and other physical boundary conditions. These models are essential to creating environmental policy and climate-resilient infrastructure; if we cannot predict the ways in which climate change will impact different regions of the world, then we will be unable to protect those who will be impacted. However, despite their importance and heavy use, these models suffer from significant forecast inaccuracies and often struggle to adequately predict the speed at which climate change is impacting the modern world (which, in turn, could delay human adaptation to this crisis). [1] Fortunately, the AI industry has begun to develop solutions. In recent years, climate scientists have begun to adapt deep neural networks (DNNs) in order to more efficiently synthesize large climatic datasets and, in turn, accurately predict the environmental consequences of climate change. Many industry experts hope that DNNs will be able to approach climate modeling from the top-down, rather than the ground-up, which will make them more capable of adapting to the novel environmental conditions brought about by climate change and more able to utilize large modern atmospheric datasets to analyze climate trends. [2] One recent project, for instance, trained a DNN on global temperature data and then used the system to forecast near-future temperature dynamics. They discovered that their model was able to accurately predict temperature changes when fed test data from the previous century and suggest that this tool can be a reliable supplement to cover the blind spots of ESMs. [3] This project, which hopes to develop a neural network capable of predicting regions that will be impacted by drought, was motivated by these recent research successes. More specifically, I hope to produce a neural network that can take in several environmental datapoints (e.g. rainfall, temperature) and accurately predict whether the described region is at risk of experiencing a near-future drought. Much of this project will likely be spent training the model with previous environmental data labeled as from a drought-impacted region of not. Several similar projects have been completed and, despite differences in the trained datasets and outputs, I'd expect to use that research as the foundation that this project builds upon. [3, 4] If successful, I'd hope to publish a conference paper (or similar) with this project.

Project Goals

  1. Develop a DNN and utilize supervised learning to train it on a dataset consisting of vectors containing environmental information (long-term rainfall, recent temperatures, etc.) and a label determining whether or not that data was collected from a region experiencing drought.
  2. Test the above DNN and, ultimately, develop a model capable of providing reasonably accurate predictions about a region's risk of drought.
  3. If our initial work is successful, increase the model's complexity by introducing additional inputs or increasing the training datasize to result in a more accurate system.
  4. In the longer term (and, again, assuming some degree of success), produce a paper outlining our work.
